Invited speaker's

Prof. Bernard Rachet (UK co-lead AMS Networking project) is a Professor in Cancer Epidemiology at the the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), UK. He leads the Inequalities in Cancer Outcomes Network (ICON) group, overseeing a wide range of projects that quantify and explain socio-demographic and geographic inequalities in cancer care and outcomes. His recent research increasingly integrates interdisciplinary approaches to assess the impact of policies on these inequalities, in collaboration with research partners across the UK and worldwide. Patient and Public Involvement has also become central to his research. Another key focus is the development and refinement of analytical methods and tools to address research questions and communicate findings to policymakers and stakeholders.
Dr. Justine Nasejje (co-partner AMS Networking project) is a Lecturer in the School of Statistics and Actuarial Science at the University of the Witwatersrand (Wits), Johannesburg. Her research interests are at the intersection of survival analysis and machine learning, with a particular emphasis on developing interpretable and robust models for time-to-event data. She has published extensively on the application of classical machine learning techniques such as Random Survival Forests to survival data, and is currently advancing neural network-based survival models to enhance both their predictive accuracy and explainability. Her recent work focuses on explainable deep learning methods for maternal and child health research in Sub-Saharan Africa, contributing to the realisation of Sustainable Development Goal 3: Good Health and Well-being. Dr Nasejje’s research is deeply collaborative, involving partnerships with institutions such as the Leibniz Institute for Prevention Research and Epidemiology, BIPS (Germany), the University of Leeds (UK), the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), and several African universities. She earned her PhD in Statistics from the University of KwaZulu-Natal in April 2018, where her doctoral thesis was recognized with the prestigious Kambule Doctoral Award. Dr Nasejje is a Humboldt Research Fellow and has been an active member of the International Biometric Society since 2015. At Wits, she leads the Survival Analysis Research Group, mentoring postgraduate students and early-career researchers while fostering regional and international collaboration. Her work bridges statistical theory, computational modeling, and public health applications, with ongoing projects addressing maternal and under-five mortality, competing risks, and interpretable neural network models for survival prediction.
Dr. Edmund Njeru Njagi (Steering AMS Networking project) is Associate Professor in Quantitative Analysis of Breast Cancer Screening at the Warwick Medical School — University of Warwick in the UK. With a background in Mathematics, Njeru obtained my Master's and PhD in Biostatistics at Hasselt University in Belgium. He specialised in analysis of complex routine health data in cancer epidemiology and taught Medical Statistics at the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine. He sits on the UK's National Health Service Race and Health Observatory's Academic Reference Group and serves as Faculty for the international biennial Corsican Summer School on Modern Methods in Biostatistics and Epidemiology held at the Université de Corse Pascal Paoli in Corsica, France. He has longstanding research and training collaboration with the University of Nairobi in Kenya and has previously been Visiting Faculty on Longitudinal Data Analysis at the Division of Epidemiology and Biostatistics of Stellenbosch University in South Africa. 
Prof. Jim Todd (Steering AMS Networking project) is a Professor of Applied Biostatistics at CUHAS, Mwanza, Tanzania, emeritus Professor at LSHTM, Senior Technical Advisor, TAZAMA Project, NIMR, Mwanza, and works with several other institutions in Africa. His research interests have focused on several projects that aimed to analyse routinely collected health data. He served as a member of the editorial board of Parasite Immunology, and Tropical Medicine and International Health and a guest editor for Frontiers in Public Health. He is currently a Secretary-Treasurer of the IBS Tanzania Region. In 2018, he received the IBS Rob Kempton Award for his outstanding contributions to the development of biometry in the developing world. He is a co-investigator on the Alpha network, which is funded by Wellcome Trust and the Gates Foundation. He is the PI for the INSPIRE network, which will develop a network for supporting HDSS in East Africa. He has led a project funded by the Global Fund for AIDS, TB and Malaria, which delivered ART in health centres, and monitored the impact on health outcomes.
Prof. Michael Johnson Mahande (Steering AMS Networking project) is an accomplished Epidemiologist and Biostatistician at Kilimanjaro Christian Medical University (KCMC University), where he teaches Advanced Epidemiology, Biostatistics, and Research Methodology, and supervises MSc and PhD students. With over 20 years of experience in health systems research across developing countries, his work spans reproductive, maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health (RMNCAH), HIV prevention, family planning, and women’s cancers. Professor Mahande has led or co-led major international research and capacity-building projects including SSACAB, THRiVE, the Willows Reproductive Health Evaluation, and the NIH-funded TRECK Program. His expertise includes managing complex health data, community-based research, and contributing to national and international policy development. He has authored over 200 peer-reviewed publications and plays a key role in advancing maternal and newborn health in Tanzania and beyond. The complete list of publications is available at his Google Scolar | ORCID.

Workshop facilitators

Dr. Wende Clarence Safari (co-lead AMS Networking project) is a Tanzanian statistician working with the Inequalities in Cancer Outcomes Network (ICON) group at the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine (LSHTM), UK. Wende holds a Bachelor’s in Applied Statistics (Mzumbe University, Tanzania), a Master’s in Biostatistics (Hasselt University, Belgium), and a PhD in Statistics (Universidade da Coruña, Spain). She focuses on developing and applying advanced survival analysis methods (cure models) to optimise the use of electronic health records and patient-reported data. Her research explores inequalities in cancer outcomes by analysing diagnostic pathways. Previously, she worked at NIMR in Mwanza on HIV/AIDS surveillance data and at the Africa Health Research Institute in South Africa, studying access to primary healthcare for individuals with TB using electronic health records.
Dr. Innocent B. Mboya (co-lead AMS Networking project) is a Senior Technical Advisor at the Africa Academy for Public Health (AAPH), Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. He completed his postdoctoral researcher position in register-based epidemiology at the Department of Translational Medicine, Lund University, Malmö, Sweden. He is also a Lecturer at Kilimanjaro Christian Medical University College, Institute of Public Health, Moshi, Tanzania. Dr. Mboya accumulated over ten years of combined experience as a researcher, project coordinator, and trainer. His experience ranges from public health, epidemiology and applied biostatistics, data quality assurance, monitoring vaccine uptake and coverage (e.g., COVID-19 and childhood vaccines in Tanzania), maternal and child health (investigating predictors of adverse pregnancy outcomes), nutritional status of mothers and children, non-communicable diseases (NCDs, such as, surveys on HPV screening for cervical cancer, hypertension, diabetes, and mental health screening), monitoring HIV infection in key populations, sexual and reproductive health, and healthcare provider workload. He has experience coordinating and conducting community-based surveys, community engagement, and project monitoring and evaluation at the local and national levels. With a background in epidemiology and applied biostatistics, Dr. Mboya possesses diverse data management and analysis skills and has authored several articles and abstracts presented at national and international conferences, demonstrating his track record in scientific writing.
Dr. Neema R. Mosha (co-partner AMS Networking project) is working as a medical Statistician and a research associate within the Mwanza Intervention Trials Unit (MITU) and the Institute for Medical Information Processing, Biometry and Epidemiology, Ludwig-Maximilians University, Munich-Germany. She is a qualified Statistician and epidemiologist with over 10 years of experience teaching and conducting research, monitoring and evaluating projects and programs. She has science degree in Statistics and Mathematics from the University of Dodoma (Tanzania), a Master's degree in Epidemiology and applied Bio-statistics from KCMUCo (Tanzania) and PhD in Biostatistics from Stellenbosch University (South-Africa). Dr. Mosha has spent most of her time working with different statistical packages including STATA and R answering research objectives in a wide range of topics such as HIV/AIDS and Sexual Transmitted infections. Non-Communicable Diseases, Gender violence, and women empowerment, Health Systems, Clinical trials and currently Emerging Infectious Diseases. Her major interest is in the application of a wide range of statistical methods in related health research/program to provide insights, recommendations, advice on policy change through the use of the evidence-based approach. Further, she is keen to provide extensive support as a faculty member to the growing arena of epidemiology and biostatistics in sub-Saharan Africa.

Dr. Jacqueline Materu (co-partner AMS Networking project) is a Biostatistician and recent PhD graduate in Biostatistics, with over seven years of experience applying advanced statistical methods to health research. Her work focuses on sexual and reproductive health, sexually transmitted infections (HIV and HSV-2), and vulnerable populations, particularly adolescents and young adults. She has collaborated with leading institutions including the National Institute for Medical Research (NIMR) in Tanzania, GTU in the USA, JHPIEGO, INSERM in France, and the Africa Health Research Institute (AHRI) in South Africa, contributing to international research projects across Tanzania, Zambia, South Africa, the United States, and France. Jacqueline has authored multiple peer-reviewed publications and trained researchers and students in statistical methods and public health data analysis. During her PhD, she received prestigious research grants, including from the UK HIV Research Trust and the Early Career Grants Programme from RSTMH in partnership with NIHR, supporting her work on advanced statistical modelling of sexual and reproductive health indicators. Her research interests include longitudinal data analysis, survival analysis, and joint/copula models, with applications to health data.
Dr. Sophia Kagoye (co-partner AMS Networking project) is a Senior Research Analyst at the Institute of Global Public Health, University of Manitoba, Canada. She holds a background in Medicine, a Master’s degree in Epidemiology and Biostatistics, and a PhD in Epidemiology from Tanzania. Her doctoral research focused on long-term mortality trends among children and adolescents, utilizing Health and Demographic Surveillance System (HDSS) data at a subnational level in Tanzania. Sophia’s current work involves the analysis and modeling of Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child, and Adolescent Health (RMNCAH) indicators and Health systems in sub-Saharan Africa using routine health facility data and population data from surveys.

Ms. Leah Sanga is a Tanzanian statistician with over 8 years in global health and implementation research across low-and-middle income countries, focusing on reproductive health, HIV/AIDS, and Non-communicable diseases (NCDs). She holds a MSc in epidemiology and applied statistics from KCMUCo and massive experienced in working with large-scale health datasets including registries, electronic health records and multi-site cohorts. Her interest is producing evidence-based health solutions through high-quality, policy-relevant research and expanding cross-sector collaborations that bridge science, policy, and community needs both locally and globally.

Dr. Witness Nyamuhuma (MD) is a recent graduate in Epidemiology and Biostatistics from CUHAS. Her Master’s project focused on "Durability of first line antiretroviral therapy, switch delay and associated factors among adults living with HIV in Mwanza, Tanzania. Her research interests include HIV/AIDS, cancer, maternal and child health, and the application of advanced statistical methods in public health. Dr. Witness will lead an interactive discussion on the practicality of electronic health records, developing research questions, and selecting appropriate methods for data analysis.

Mr. Tathagata Bhattacharjee is a Data Scientist with a robust background in data management & analysis in population research. Since joining LSHTM in 2017, he has contributed significantly to major international networks like Alpha network and INSPIRE. His academic credentials include a BSc Honours in Physics and an MBA in Information Technology. He is also a passionate researcher, pursuing his PhD alongside his professional responsibilities. His professional focus includes developing data pipelines, harmonising large datasets, and leveraging advanced data science tools to enhance population research and policy.
Mr. David Amadi is a Data Documentalist with background in public health informatics and extensive expertise in research data management, metadata standards, and the implementation of FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable) principles. His work focuses on transforming complex research data into machine-readable, interoperable formats and developing metadata frameworks to optimise data sharing, accessibility, and reuse. David has contributed to major international research initiatives, including the INDEPTH Network and currently the INSPIRE Network, where he supports the FAIR sharing of longitudinal population health data. He is highly skilled in creating Implementation Guides (IGs) for studies, managing standardised data catalog systems, and ensuring compliance with global metadata standards. Beyond his documentation work, David applies his data science expertise to design and implement data pipelines, harmonise and standardise health data, and deploy the OMOP Common Data Model (CDM). Collaborating with engineers and data scientists, he develops Extract, Transform, and Load (ETL) processes aligned with international standards, supporting reliable and comprehensive data integration for global health research
Dr. Philip Ayieko (co-partner AMS Networking project) is an Assistant Professor of Medical Statistics and Epidemiology at LSHTM, and is based fulltime in Mwanza. He has an interest in the design and analysis of clinical trials and has previously designed and analysed cluster randomised trials on child health interventions. Prior to joining the School and the Mwanza Intervention Trials Unit, Philip worked at the Kenya Medical Research Institute-Wellcome Trust programme since 2005 and his areas of work included systematic reviews in child health, costs of treatment for paediatric diseases and cost-effectiveness analysis of vaccination, and quality of hospital care in paediatrics with a focus on pneumonia. Between 2013 and 2017, he served as the lead epidemiologist of the Kenyan clinical information network, a collaborative project between hospitals, Ministry of Health, academic institutions and professional bodies that aims to use hospital data to improve quality of paediatric care.

Dr. Bonny Mgawe is a lecturer at the Nelson Mandela African Institution of Science and Technology (NM-AIST) and a Certified Telecommunication Engineer (Level 3, Registration No. P0656-RFC). He has been involved in several impactful projects. One of his notable collaborations was with King Mongkut’s University of Technology Thonburi (Thailand), where he helped design and develop a device that measures soil health using antennas. This innovation aims to support small-scale farmers by providing a cost-effective alternative to traditional agricultural laboratory testing, thereby promoting modern farming techniques and improving crop yields. Currently, he is actively involved in two national strategic initiatives. The first is the Tanzania Internalisation of Higher Education (TANZIE) project, which seeks to strengthen the international profile and competitiveness of Tanzanian higher education institutions by promoting academic exchange, research collaboration, and institutional capacity building. The second is the Assessment of Artificial Intelligence (AI) Readiness towards the Development of the National AI Strategy in Tanzania, a foundational project aimed at evaluating the country's preparedness for AI adoption and contributing to the formulation of a comprehensive national AI strategy.
